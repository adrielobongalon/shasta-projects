Cows - Methane

Moo.

Cows. Yes, Cows. Cattle. Those farm animals that go "moo". You might not believe me, but they are actually a major source of air pollution.

You see, with a huge populations comes a huge demand for beef (and milk). And in order to get all that beef, we need to raise a lot of cattle and make sure they grow big. To increase farming efficiency, we feed them a certain type of grass, but they aren't able to fully digest it. It ferments in their stomachs, producing gas that they release into the atmosphere by burping. The problem is that one of those gases is methane (CH4), a greenhouse gas.

So to answer my initial question of how many litres of methane get released by cows in the US every year, I needed to research how much and how often cows release methane, as well as the number of cows in the US. According to How Stuff Works, experts estimate that a single dairy cow expels between 100 and 500 litres of methane per day, an "amount comparable to the pollution produced by a car". And according to the National Agricultural Statistics Service from the US Department of Agriculture, as of January 1, 2016, the US Cattle Inventory included 91,988,000 cattle.

And from there, it was just maths. Now keep in mind that since I was given a range for the methane output of a cow, I made seperate calculations for both the low end and the high end.

So annually, cows from the US produce somewhere between roughly 3 and 17 trillion litres of methane. The "per US" unit might seem a little bit awkward, but it was necessary because of the conversion factors. Since there is only one United States of America, the unit can essentially be thought of as just "litres per year".

Assumptions and Limitations

There are a multitude of issues with this answer. First of all, it assumes that the number of cows in the US remain the same. It also assumes that each cow produces a constant amount of methane. Obviously, the number of cows is likely to fluctuate, and diffrent cows will produce different amount of methane. Further, what classifies as a "cow" affects the number. The USDA figure I used in my calculation includes "beef cows, milk cows, bulls, replacement heifers, other steers and heifers, and . . . calves", according to the Guide to NASS Surveys. Whether animals such as bulls are considered cows may be up for debate, and for those who think they are not, a different figure would be necessary do make the calculations. Further, the figures are rounded, so this would mathematically skew the figures by a relatively small amount.

Most importantly, it should be noted that measuring gases in terms of volume doesn't really tell us anything about it because gases can always be compressed or expanded by changing the size of their containers. For example, a kilogram of methane can take up the space of an entire house, but it can also be compressed in a container into the size of a small box. Because of this, whenever gases are given in units of volume (instead of mass), it is important to include additional information such as the pressure or the temperature.

The "100 to 500 letres per day" statistic came from How Stuff Works, which seems to have synthesised the statistic from The Guardian (which reports between 100 and 200 litres) and some other source that I was unable to trace back to. I looked through all the references the author listed, but was unable to find the 500 litre statistic, although this may be because many of the sources are no longer available at the original source location nor the Wayback Machine Internet Archive. In any case, none of the sources that listed the daily methane output in terms of volume specified any temperature or pressure, so it remains unclear as to exactly how much methane exists within each unit of volume. The only figures in units of mass I was able to locate was between 70 and 120 kg per year, but the reliability of the source is questionable. Thus, I had to do what I could with the information I had.

Summarily, this estimate should be taken with a grain of salt, and it isn't in a particularly useful unit of measurement.
