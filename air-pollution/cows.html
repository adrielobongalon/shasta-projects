Cows - Methane

Moo.

Cows. Yes, Cows. Cattle. Those farm animals that go "moo". You might not believe me, but they are actually a major source of air pollution.

You see, with a huge populations comes a huge demand for beef (and milk). And in order to get all that beef, we need to raise a lot of cattle and make sure they grow big. To increase farming efficiency, we feed them a certain type of grass, but they aren't able to fully digest it. It ferments in their stomachs, producing gas that they release into the atmosphere by burping. The problem is that one of those gases is methane (CH4), a greenhouse gas.

So to answer my initial question of how many litres of methane get released by cows in the US every year, I needed to research how much and how often cows release methane, as well as the number of cows in the US. According to How Stuff Works, experts estimate that a single dairy cow expels between 100 and 500 litres of methane per day, an "amount comparable to the pollution produced by a car". And according to the National Agricultural Statistics Service from the US Department of Agriculture, as of January 1, 2016, the US Cattle Inventory included 91,988,000 cattle.

And from there, it was just maths. Now keep in mind that since I was given a range for the methane output of a cow, I made seperate calculations for both the low end and the high end.
